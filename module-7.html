Opening

Busy Isn't the Same as Building

Most men mistake activity for progress. They are not the same thing.

You have been auditing your identity, mapping power, and building leverage. All of that work is inputs. Strategy is what determines what you do with them — what you're actually moving toward, by what route, and by when.

Without strategy, effort disperses. You make progress in multiple directions and arrive nowhere in particular. You handle what appears in front of you, respond to other people's agendas, stay busy, and then look up five years later wondering why your position hasn't fundamentally changed despite how hard you've worked.

The problem was never effort. The problem was the absence of a chosen direction — and the discipline to hold it.

Strategy is not a business school concept. It is the answer to a simple question: given where I am and where I want to be, what is the sequence of moves that gets me there most efficiently — and which moves do I need to stop making because they consume resources without building position?

Sun Tzu's central insight was not about tactics. It was about positioning. Win before the battle, not during it. The general who wins is the one who has already arranged things so that his opponent has no good options before the fighting starts. The tactician reacts to the battlefield. The strategist designs the battlefield.

This module is about shifting from tactical to strategic — from responding to what appears to moving toward what you've decided.

The Hard Truth

You Don't Have a Direction Problem

You have a commitment problem. Most men know what they should be building. They just won't decide.

Hard Truth #7

"A man without a strategy isn't building his life — he's reacting to it. Busy men with no direction work hard and stay exactly where they are."

The discomfort of committing to a direction is real. Once you decide, you are also deciding not to pursue the alternatives. You are declaring a bet. You are making yourself accountable to a specific outcome. That feels risky — and so most men avoid it by staying in perpetual exploration, always considering options, always keeping everything open.

The cost of this is enormous and mostly invisible. Every month without a direction is a month of effort that builds no position. Every year without a strategy is a year of compound interest you didn't collect. The man who commits to a direction at 25 and holds it has a fundamentally different life at 35 than the man who is still figuring it out.

The false safety of optionality

There is a version of "keeping options open" that is genuine strategic flexibility — staying uncommitted until you have enough information to make a good decision. That is sound. But most men are not practising strategic flexibility. They are avoiding commitment because commitment feels like foreclosure.

Uncommitted men don't have more options. They have weaker positions in all of them. The man who commits and moves accumulates experience, reputation, and leverage in a specific direction. The man who keeps all options open accumulates nothing in particular. By the time he decides, the other man already has a position he cannot quickly replicate.

The exploration trap: If you have been "figuring out your direction" for more than 12 months, you are not exploring — you are avoiding. The direction doesn't have to be perfect. It has to be chosen. You can adjust as you go. You cannot build from a standing start.

The Lesson

How Strategy Actually Works

Not a plan. Not a vision board. A coherent sequence of moves that builds a specific position over time.

Richard Rumelt's definition of good strategy is precise: it identifies a challenge, names the approach for addressing it, and aligns the specific actions behind that approach. Bad strategy, by contrast, is goals dressed up as strategy — "I want to be financially free" is not a strategy, it is a preference. A strategy answers how.

For our purposes, strategy has four components that need to be working together:

1. A chosen position

Where do you want to be in three to five years — specifically? Not "successful" or "free." A specific position: a particular income level, a particular type of work, a particular kind of relationship, a particular place to live. Without a specific target, all effort is equally valid and equally wasteful. The target doesn't have to be certain. It has to be chosen.

2. An honest current state

Strategy requires an accurate map of where you are now — not where you'd like to be, not where you thought you'd be by now. Modules 1 through 6 have been building this map: your character, your beliefs, your leverage, your power position. The gap between current state and target position is where your strategy lives.

3. A sequenced set of moves

The bridge between where you are and where you want to be is built one move at a time — and the sequence matters. Not every move is available now. Some require capabilities you don't yet have. Some require positions you haven't yet built. Strategy is knowing which moves to make first, in what order, to unlock the next set of moves. This is what separates men who progress from men who spin their wheels: not effort, but sequencing.

4. The things you stop doing

This is the part most men omit. Strategy is as much about what you choose not to do as what you choose to do. Every hour spent on a low-leverage activity is an hour not spent building position. Every commitment that doesn't serve the direction is dead weight. Good strategy requires deliberate subtraction — not just addition.

Second-order thinking: before any significant decision, ask what happens next. And then what happens after that. Most men think one move ahead. Strategic men think three. The second-order consequence of a decision is often more important than the first-order effect — and it is rarely visible to someone who isn't looking for it.

The Game Question — What position are you actually trying to build over the next 3–5 years? Not a feeling or a lifestyle aesthetic — a specific, concrete description of where you want to be. Be precise about income, work, relationships, location, and what a typical day looks like.

Question 03

The Asymmetric Move — What is the one move available to you right now where the cost of attempting is relatively low but the potential upside — the change to your position if it works — is disproportionately large? What has stopped you from making it?

Asymmetric moves are the engine of strategic progress. A small, low-risk bet with a large potential return — a new skill, a conversation you haven't had, an application you haven't submitted, a project you haven't started. Most men have at least one obvious asymmetric move they've been avoiding. Name it.

"The asymmetric move I've been avoiding — low cost to attempt, potentially large upside — is..."

"If it worked, the change to my position would be..."

"The real reason I haven't done it yet is... (and whether that reason is valid is...)"

Question 04

04

The Stop List — What are you doing right now — commitments, habits, relationships, projects — that consume real time and energy but are not building toward the position you described in Question 1? What would you lose by stopping, and is that loss real or imagined?

Every strategy requires a stop list. Not just a list of things to start. The resources you free by stopping low-leverage activities are the resources that fund the things you actually want to build. Most men find this harder than adding new commitments, because stopping requires confronting sunk costs and disappointing people.

"The things I am currently doing that are not building toward my chosen position are..."

"If I stopped [X], the real cost would be... and whether I can afford that cost is..."

"The thing I am most reluctant to stop — and the honest reason why — is..."

Question 05

05

The Second-Order Question — Think of the most significant decision you are currently facing or have recently made. What is the first-order consequence? Now go deeper: what happens as a result of that? And then what? What would someone with a 10-year time horizon see that your current thinking is missing?

Most regret comes from not thinking past the immediate consequence. The promotion that locks you into a role you hate. The relationship you stayed in because leaving was uncomfortable. The investment that made sense in isolation. Second-order thinking is a skill — it requires deliberately slowing down and asking "and then what?" at least twice before deciding.

"The decision I'm currently facing is... The first-order consequence is..."

"But what happens after that? And then what? The third-order consequence I haven't thought about is..."

"What someone with a 10-year time horizon would see that I'm missing is..."

The System

The Strategic Map

Map your current position, target position, key moves, and obstacles across four domains. This is the document you return to every 90 days.

A strategy is a working document, not a declaration. Write it specifically enough that you'd be able to look at it in 90 days and honestly assess whether you moved toward it or not. Vague strategies produce vague accountability.

The Exercise

The Timeline Builder

Map the specific position you're targeting at each time horizon — and the key move required to reach it. Work backwards from the longest horizon to the shortest.

Start at the 3-year horizon and work backwards. The 3-year target determines what the 1-year milestone needs to be. The 1-year milestone determines what needs to happen in the next 90 days. The 90-day goal determines this week's priority. Strategy runs backwards; execution runs forwards.

Weekly Challenge

The 90-Day Plan

Do this before moving to Module 8. This is the most important output of this module.

Write a specific 90-day strategic plan: three positions to build, three key moves to make, and three things to stop doing. One page. Specific enough to be falsifiable.

A 90-day horizon is long enough to see real progress and short enough to hold attention. The plan does not need to be comprehensive or perfect — it needs to be honest and specific. Three positions, not ten. Three moves, not a wishlist. Three stops, not a vague intention to "cut back."

The test of a good 90-day plan: if you looked at it in 90 days, you would be able to say clearly whether you had done it or not. Vague plans produce vague accountability. Write it so that there is no ambiguity about whether you moved or didn't.

After you write it, share it with one person — not for approval, but for accountability. The act of stating a direction to someone else is significantly more binding than stating it only to yourself.

Three Positions to Build — in the next 90 days

What specific, measurable state do you want to be in 90 days from now in the three areas that matter most? Use concrete language: numbers, behaviours, outcomes — not feelings.

Go Deeper

Assigned Reading

For Module 7. These are the clearest treatments of strategic thinking available — each addresses a different dimension of how direction is chosen and held.

Primary

Good Strategy Bad Strategy — Richard Rumelt

The most rigorous and readable book on what strategy actually is. Rumelt's distinction between good strategy (a specific challenge + a guiding policy + coherent actions) and bad strategy (goals and wishful thinking dressed up as strategy) is essential reading for anyone who has ever confused ambition with direction. Part I alone is worth the read.

Read

The Art of War — Sun Tzu

Read for the second time — now through the lens of Module 7. The strategic insight is not about battles. It is about positioning: arranging conditions so that the outcome is determined before the conflict begins. The chapter on strategic positioning (Chapter 4) and the chapter on adaptation (Chapter 8) are particularly relevant here.

Read

Thinking in Bets — Annie Duke

Duke's framework for separating decision quality from outcome quality is directly relevant to strategic thinking. Good decisions sometimes produce bad outcomes. Bad decisions sometimes produce good outcomes. Learning to evaluate your strategic choices by the quality of your thinking at the time — not by how they turn out — is how you improve your decision-making over time rather than just getting lucky.

Read

The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People — Stephen Covey (Habits 2 & 3)

Read selectively: Habit 2 (Begin with the End in Mind) is the clearest formulation of working backwards from a chosen destination. Habit 3 (Put First Things First) addresses the reactive vs. proactive distinction directly — specifically the time management matrix that separates what is urgent from what is important. These two chapters are operationally useful and complement Rumelt directly.
