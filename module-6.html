Opening

Why Most Men Stay Replaceable

Effort is common. Leverage is rare. The gap between them is where most lives are quietly decided.

In Module 5, you mapped the power structures around you — the rooms, the dynamics, where you sit and where the influence actually flows. Now comes the harder question: what do you have that changes your position in those structures?

Most men work hard. Most men show up. Most men do what they're told, deliver what's expected, and then wonder why their situation doesn't change as fast as their effort would suggest it should. The answer is almost always the same: effort without leverage is just labour. And labour gets priced like a commodity, because it is one.

Leverage is the specific thing — a skill, a resource, a relationship, a piece of knowledge — that makes you genuinely hard to replace. It is what changes the terms of your negotiations, your career, your earning power, your relationships. Without it, you are always subject to other people's decisions. With it, you start making your own.

This is not abstract. The man who has one income stream and a common skill set is at the mercy of whoever employs him. The man who has three income streams, a rare skill, and access to a network of people who matter is negotiating from a fundamentally different position. Same hours in the day. Very different leverage.

The difference between them was not intelligence or luck. It was deliberate construction. One man built something. The other waited for something to be given.

This module is about the construction. Where leverage actually comes from, how to map what you currently have, and what to build next.

The Hard Truth

Common Is Cheap

The market is ruthlessly honest about what you're actually worth in it.

Hard Truth #6

"Skills that everyone has are worth what everyone gets. Build the specific thing that makes you irreplaceable — or spend your life accepting what you're given."

The labour market, the social market, the relationship market — every market you participate in prices you by how easily you can be replaced. If ten other people can do what you do, at your level, you will be paid like ten other people. If no one else can do what you do, in the way you can do it, you will be paid like no one else.

This is not cruel. It is simply how scarcity and value work. And it has a direct implication: the most important question you can ask about your position in any market is not "am I good?" but "am I rare?"

The comfort trap

There is a specific way competence becomes a trap. You become good enough to be kept, but not rare enough to be irreplaceable. You get enough reward to feel like progress but not enough to feel like freedom. You become comfortable in a position you can't leverage. You have a job, not a position.

Comfort at the level of competence is one of the most effective ways to prevent the development of leverage. The man who is good enough to keep doesn't feel the pressure to become rare. He is neither desperate enough to change nor free enough to not have to.

The replacement test: If you disappeared from your job, your client list, your professional network tomorrow — how long would it take to replace you? Days? Weeks? If the honest answer is "not long," you have a leverage problem. Not a work ethic problem. A leverage problem.

Being hard to replace is not about being difficult or withholding. It is about building something specific, rare, and genuinely valuable — and then making sure the people who matter know you have it.

The Lesson

Three Forms of Leverage

Not all leverage is equal. Understanding which form to build — and in what order — determines how fast your position changes.

Leverage is not one thing. There are three distinct forms, each building differently, each with its own timeline and risk profile. Most men have partial access to one. The men who change their position fastest are deliberately building all three.

Skill leverage — the foundation

Skill leverage is what you can do that few others can do at your level. It is the most direct form of leverage and the most within your control. You build it by going deeper than the market goes — by specialising in ways that create genuine scarcity rather than staying generalist in ways that keep you fungible.

The key insight from Cal Newport's work: rare and valuable skills require rare and valuable practice. The man who spends his time doing what is easy to him builds competence. The man who deliberately stretches into the uncomfortable edge of his capability builds leverage. These feel different from the inside. The first feels productive. The second feels like struggle. One builds a career. The other builds a position.

The Rarity Question

For any skill you're developing: could most people in your field claim this at roughly your level? If yes, it is a competence, not leverage. Leverage requires going further than almost everyone in your field is willing to go — in a specific direction that the market values.

Resource leverage — money working for you

Resource leverage is what happens when your assets generate more options than your labour alone can generate. A man with savings has more negotiating power than a man without, because he can walk away from bad deals. A man with multiple income streams cannot be controlled by a single employer. A man with owned assets has a floor that labour alone cannot provide.

The first rule of resource leverage: stop trading time for money as your only mechanism. Skill leverage unlocks higher time value. But it still trades time. Resource leverage begins when some of your income arrives whether or not you show up. That shift — even a small one — changes your negotiating position in everything.

Network leverage — access through association

Network leverage is access — to information, opportunities, capital, introductions, and trust that exist inside relationships rather than in the open market. Most of what moves at the upper levels of any field moves through networks, not job boards. The right introduction is worth years of independent effort.

The mistake most men make with networking is treating it as transactional — going to events, collecting contacts, asking for favours. Network leverage is built differently: by becoming genuinely worth knowing, by offering value before asking for it, and by investing in a small number of relationships with disproportionate depth rather than a large number of relationships with no depth at all.

The compounding effect: skill leverage raises your value in the market. Resource leverage reduces your dependency on any single part of it. Network leverage opens access to parts of the market that aren't publicly available. Built together, they create a position that is genuinely hard to displace — not because you're protected, but because you've made yourself expensive to replace and easy to work with.

The System

The Leverage Audit

Map your current leverage position across four domains. For each: what you have, what it's worth in the real world, where the biggest gap is, and what to build next.

Be honest about what is actual leverage and what is just competence. Competence is the baseline. Leverage is what's rare at your level of competence. You're not looking for what you're good at — you're looking for what would be genuinely hard to replace.

The Exercise

The Skill Stack

Rate your current skill level across five key categories. Then identify the one to build most urgently — and map what that actually requires.

Most men are specialists in one area and weak everywhere else. The highest-leverage individuals have a stack — a core rare skill combined with enough business, communication, and technical capability to deploy it effectively. Rate honestly.

Weekly Challenge

The Leverage Build

Do this before you move to Module 7. This challenge starts a habit that compounds across years.

For the next seven days, commit 30 uninterrupted minutes per day to building the specific leverage asset you identified in Reflection Question 4. No exceptions. No substitutions.

30 minutes per day is not a large commitment. But 30 minutes of deliberate, focused skill-building — compounded daily, weekly, yearly — is how rare capability is actually built. Not through heroic multi-hour sessions that are unsustainable, but through consistent, uncomfortable practice in the specific direction that creates scarcity.

The rules: it must be deliberate practice, not comfortable practice. If it feels easy, you are reinforcing competence, not building leverage. Seek the edge of your current ability and work there. This is where the market-moving skills are built.

At the end of each session, write down what you worked on, what felt hard, and what you noticed about the edge of your current capability. Over seven days, patterns will emerge about where your leverage actually is and where it isn't yet.

Go Deeper

Assigned Reading

For Module 6. These are the books that most directly address how leverage is built — not theoretically, but operationally.

Primary

So Good They Can't Ignore You — Cal Newport

The most rigorous treatment of how rare and valuable career capital is actually built. Newport's central argument — that "follow your passion" is bad advice, and that passion follows mastery — is directly relevant here. The sections on deliberate practice and career capital are the operational framework for this module's challenge. Read the whole book, but focus on Part II and III.

Read

The Almanack of Naval Ravikant — Eric Jorgenson

Naval's framework for building wealth without renting out your time is one of the clearest articulations of leverage available. His distinction between permissioned and permissionless leverage — and why specific knowledge (what you know that cannot be taught in a course) is more valuable than general skill — maps directly onto the three forms of leverage in this module.

Read

Never Eat Alone — Keith Ferrazzi

The most practical treatment of network leverage available. Ferrazzi's core insight — that the most valuable networkers are relentless givers, not strategic takers — reframes how relationship capital is built. The chapters on how to approach people who are above you in any hierarchy are particularly relevant for the Network Audit.

Watch

Naval Ravikant — How to Get Rich (Not a How-To) (podcast / transcript)

The original Twitter thread expanded into a full conversation. The sections on specific knowledge, permissionless leverage, and the difference between renting your time and owning equity in something are essential listening for anyone building the leverage side of Module 6. Available free as a podcast episode or full transcript online.
